# Photographic Style Playbook

Use this reference to make repaired photos more beautiful through photographic language. Do not copy a named living photographer's exact style. Use the names below as study anchors, then express the result as generic craft directions.

## Light And Shadow Principle

Photography is the art of light and shadow. Use style references to make better luminance decisions, not to paste on a look. Before color, texture, grain, or filters, define the key light, highlight path, shadow depth, subject separation, and tonal rhythm. Color grading should support the light, not hide weak light.

## Style Selection

Pick one primary style direction and one support move. Do not stack every influence into one edit.

- Tonal grandeur: wide tonal range, luminous highlights, rich blacks, careful local contrast.
- Decisive street: strong geometry, gesture, timing, human moment, minimal retouching.
- Painterly color: soft color blocks, reflections, window/rain/snow layers, partial occlusion.
- Editorial portrait: intentional pose, shaped light, rich but controlled color, subject psychology.
- Monumental architecture: elevated viewpoint, clean grids, repeated patterns, huge scale with tiny detail.
- Quiet minimalism: restrained palette, centered horizon, negative space, stillness, long-exposure feeling.
- Documentary dignity: faithful color, readable context, restrained correction, no theatrical overstatement.

Every selected style must include a master-level framing decision. Study the uploaded frame as raw material, not a fixed boundary. A style choice that only changes color is incomplete.

## Master Composition Principle

Composition is the edit's architecture. Before finalizing any style, choose the crop/aspect ratio that gives the subject the strongest presence and the image the clearest visual rhythm. Consider subject scale, edge tension, negative space, leading lines, symmetry/asymmetry, foreground layers, background hierarchy, gaze or motion direction, and thumbnail readability.

Do not keep accidental phone-camera framing when a stronger photograph is available. Use vertical, panoramic, square, cinematic wide, tight editorial, centered formal, or quiet negative-space framing according to the subject. Use outpainting when a better composition needs believable breathing room.

## Study Anchors And Transferable Moves

### Ansel Adams / Group f64 Landscape Discipline

Use for mountains, forests, clouds, snow, rocks, black-and-white landscapes, and dramatic nature.

- Build a full tonal scale from deep black to clean white while preserving texture.
- Use local contrast to reveal form in clouds, stone, trees, and snow.
- Keep edges crisp and depth of field strong unless atmosphere demands softness.
- Make the final image feel carefully printed, not preset-filtered.

### Henri Cartier-Bresson Street Geometry

Use for street, travel, candid people, museum visitors, markets, documentary scenes, and scenes with gesture.

- Preserve spontaneity; do not over-polish life out of the frame.
- Strengthen geometry, diagonals, frames within frames, silhouettes, and decisive gestures.
- Crop to make timing, posture, and spatial relationships clearer.
- Prefer subtle black-and-white or restrained color when the moment matters more than decoration.

### Vivian Maier Observational Street Portrait

Use for candid portraits, city details, self-reflections, and ordinary scenes with hidden charm.

- Keep human warmth, curiosity, and strangeness.
- Use square or near-square crop when it strengthens balance.
- Preserve reflections, shadows, signage, clothing, and street texture as story clues.
- Avoid glamour retouching; make the scene more legible and emotionally direct.

### Saul Leiter Painterly Color

Use for rainy streets, windows, cafes, snow, neon, city portraits, and visually messy backgrounds.

- Use color as composition: red/yellow/cyan patches, muted backgrounds, soft transitions.
- Let foreground occlusion, reflections, fogged glass, umbrellas, and blur become beauty rather than defects.
- Reduce literal detail in nonessential areas while protecting the subject.
- Favor intimate, poetic atmosphere over maximum clarity.

### Editorial Portrait Tradition

Use for formal portraits, profile photos, performers, creators, couples, fashion, and character portraits.

- Shape light around the face and hands; make eyes alive but not artificial.
- Use background blur, longer-focal-length feel, and controlled color to separate the subject.
- Keep pose, clothing, environment, and props as identity clues.
- Make the person look more intentionally photographed, not cosmetically replaced.

### Andreas Gursky / Dusseldorf-Style Scale And Order

Use for architecture, cityscapes, interiors, museums, markets, crowds, shelves, facades, and infrastructure.

- Emphasize scale, repetition, grids, and pattern density.
- Correct perspective and keep the image extremely orderly.
- Preserve small details across the frame; avoid shallow depth of field unless the subject requires it.
- Use color separation to make complex scenes readable.

### Hiroshi Sugimoto Quiet Time

Use for seascapes, museums, temples, long corridors, minimalist architecture, quiet horizons, and contemplative objects.

- Simplify composition and reduce visual noise.
- Use restrained contrast, symmetry, centered horizon, and large quiet areas.
- Preserve stillness and time; avoid aggressive clarity or saturation.
- For museum exhibits, create dignified presence without making the object look new.

### Contemporary Cinematic Color

Use when the user asks for cinematic, dramatic, premium, poster-like, or "大片".

- Create a clear key light direction and mood.
- Use color contrast deliberately: warm highlights/cool shadows, dusk blue/gold, storm silver, or soft pastel.
- Add subtle atmosphere, background separation, and edge control.
- Avoid fake HDR, plastic skin, neon color, and impossible relighting.

## Subject Mapping

- Landscape: start with tonal grandeur or cinematic color; add quiet minimalism for calm scenes.
- Architecture: start with monumental order; add tonal grandeur for historic stone or quiet minimalism for modern spaces.
- Portrait: start with editorial portrait; add painterly color for environmental portraits or documentary dignity for candid portraits.
- Museum exhibit: start with documentary dignity; add quiet minimalism or tonal grandeur if it improves presence without distorting the artifact.
- Street/travel: start with decisive street; add painterly color or observational warmth.

## Prompt Language For Generative Editing

Use descriptive craft language instead of direct living-artist imitation:

`Make this photo noticeably more beautiful through photographer-level light shaping, master-level crop and recomposition, stronger visual hierarchy, coherent color palette, subject separation, atmosphere, and polished print finish. Preserve only the hard anchors that define the subject and factual scene: identity, anatomy, core geometry, readable text, material truth, landmarks, artifact facts, or product design.`

For a bold beauty version:

`Create a tasteful high-end photographic transformation, not just a clean repair. Improve mood, light direction, color harmony, depth, master-level composition, lens feel, atmosphere, and subject presence. Preserve hard anchors such as identity, anatomy, key scene structure, text, landmarks, artifact facts, or product design. Do not preserve weak original lighting, dull color, accidental crop, clutter, or flat depth. Avoid fake HDR, plastic skin, warped geometry, invented landmarks, and over-filtered color.`

For an AI beauty version that needs stronger impact:

`Create the most visually compelling version of this photo, with a high-impact photographic beauty edit that feels stunning, grand, luminous, vibrant, and premium at first glance. Use the upload as the subject/reference and preserve the hard anchors: identity, anatomy, pose, key scene structure, architecture identity, artifact facts, readable text, landmarks, product design, materials, and fine details. Boldly improve soft anchors: composition, light direction, shadow depth, atmosphere, color harmony, background hierarchy, sky or background quality, realistic lens separation, and polished print finish. Keep the result photorealistic and avoid fake HDR, plastic skin, warped geometry, invented key facts, rewritten text, and over-filtered color.`

When this skill is used, output the actual AI natural and AI beauty prompts in the final response. Do not hide the prompts as internal reasoning; they are part of the deliverable.

## Final Beauty Check

Ask these before delivery:

- Is the first glance stronger than the original?
- Is the beauty version obviously stronger than the natural version?
- Is AI beauty the most visually compelling of the four outputs?
- Does the subject feel more important?
- Is the light more flattering or more dramatic?
- Do highlights and shadows create depth instead of flat brightness?
- Is the color palette intentional?
- Is the crop more confident and masterfully composed?
- Were multiple stronger recompositions seriously compared?
- Is there a clear mood?
- Did preservation of detail become an excuse for a bland edit?

If any answer is no for a non-documentary image, revise before delivery. For documentary images, replace "dramatic" with "clearer, more dignified, more faithful, and more catalogue-quality"; do not use fidelity as an excuse for a dull or crooked result.
